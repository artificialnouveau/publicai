The Slow Boil Has Already Started

In Dublin, OpenAI just gutted its European offices. Aaron, a policy advisor for seven years, walked out with a cardboard box and a hard drive. The Munich office, the Paris office, the Brussels office — all decimated. The engineering went back to San Francisco.

In Brussels, Henna Virkkunen threw out her prepared remarks and told a room full of delegates what nobody wanted to hear: "We are toothless regulators over technology that doesn't even belong to us."

In Berlin, Friedrich Merz is reading a document that will change everything. But he doesn't know that yet.

Ronald Coase explained why firms exist: when it is cheaper to organize inside a hierarchy than through constant market bargaining. AI requires an unusually high degree of coordination amongst tightly complementary pieces. The US solves this with gigantic capital pools. China has giant firms with coordinated state control. The EU has 27 countries, 24 languages, diluted capital, and zero sense of centralized execution.

✈ THE AIRBUS PARALLEL
In the late 1960s, Boeing owned commercial aviation. Individual European manufacturers were all too small to compete alone. Each had capability, but none had the full stack.

France, Germany, the UK, and Spain didn't want to merge their aerospace industries. They did it because the alternative was worse. The first decade was full of fights over workshare, headquarters, whose engine gets used. It worked despite the friction. Today Airbus holds 50% of the global commercial aviation market.

✈ THE AIRBUS PARALLEL
Airbus didn't start with vision. It started with everyone running out of alternatives at the same time. Sound familiar?

By 1967, each European aircraft manufacturer had independently concluded it couldn't afford the next generation of wide-body jets. The coalition was born from synchronized desperation, not synchronized ambition. Merz calling Macron at 5am. Virkkunen throwing out her prepared remarks. Takaichi reaching out through backchannels. The pattern is the same.

✈ THE AIRBUS PARALLEL
By 1975 Airbus had sold 38 planes. Boeing sold over 1,500 that decade. The early years were humiliating. But the structure held.

The A300 was a commercial disappointment for its first five years. Airlines wouldn't buy European. But the consortium kept iterating, and by the 1980s had real customers. Today Airbus holds ~50% of the global commercial aviation market.
