4.23.2026 — Dublin, Ireland — OpenAI Offices

Chapter I: Dublin

Aaron noticed the calendar invite at 8:47 AM. It was from someone in HR he had never met, titled simply "Sync." No agenda. No context. Fifteen minutes, blocked at 11:00.

He knew what it was. Everyone in the Dublin office knew. The rumors had been circulating for weeks, accelerating in the last forty-eight hours as Slack channels went quiet one by one, replaced by DMs that stopped mid-sentence. The phrase "rebalancing toward core research hubs" had appeared in an all-hands slide the previous Friday. Nobody in Dublin was a core research hub.

Aaron spent the next two hours doing what anyone does when they know. He didn't work. He didn't panic. He sat at his desk in the open-plan fourth floor of the Barrow Street office, looking out at the Grand Canal Dock, and he thought about the three years he had spent building something that was about to be taken away from him by someone he had never spoken to.

He was a senior infrastructure engineer. His team — seven people, five nationalities, all European residents — had built the model evaluation pipeline that OpenAI used to benchmark every major release since GPT-4.5. They had done this from Dublin because Dublin was supposed to be the European hub. That was the pitch when he was recruited from Intercom in 2023: "We're building real engineering capacity in Europe, not just a sales office."

The meeting lasted eleven minutes. The HR person was in San Francisco, appearing on his screen at 7 PM her time. She was kind, in the way that people are kind when they have been trained to be kind in these situations. There was a package. There was a timeline. There were resources for "transition support." Aaron's badge would be deactivated at 5 PM Dublin time.

"Can I ask something?" Aaron said. "The evaluation pipeline. Who's taking it over?"

A pause. "That's being handled by the SF team going forward."

"They don't know how it works. We wrote all the tooling for the EU compliance layer. The GDPR integration, the data residency checks — that's all custom. It's not documented."

Another pause. "I understand. We'll make sure there's a knowledge transfer process."

There was no knowledge transfer process. Aaron knew this because he had seen three other teams shut down the same way in the past eighteen months. The Zurich safety team. The London policy team. The Berlin partnerships team. Each time, the official line was consolidation. Each time, the institutional knowledge — the European institutional knowledge, the understanding of how things actually work across twenty-seven regulatory jurisdictions — simply evaporated.

At 4:30 PM, Aaron was in the parking garage with Katya, his team lead, who had received the same calendar invite at the same time. She was loading a box into her car. They stood there, in the concrete quiet, and she handed him a hard drive.

"What's this?" he asked.

"Everything we built. The evaluation framework, the compliance tools, the multilingual benchmarks. All of it."

"Katya, that's — we can't just—"

"It's our work. We wrote it. And nobody in San Francisco is going to maintain the EU compliance layer. In six months, they'll rebuild it badly, or they'll just stop doing compliance testing in European languages. You know that."

He did know that. He took the drive.

Across the city, forty-three other people were having variations of the same day. OpenAI's Dublin office was not closing — it was being "restructured into a commercial hub." The engineering teams were gone. The research capacity was gone. What remained was sales, partnerships, and government relations. A storefront, not a factory.

Aaron drove home along the M50 and thought about what Katya had said in the parking garage, the last thing before she got in her car:

"We're not losing jobs, Aaron. We're losing capability. And nobody's counting."

The Argument

Chapter II: Economics 101

Go back to the source. Ronald Coase: firms exist when it is cheaper to organize activity inside a hierarchy than through constant market bargaining.

This is the most important sentence in the entire debate about European AI sovereignty. Not the technical arguments about model architectures. Not the geopolitical arguments about strategic autonomy. The economics.

This is the central sin of the middle powers. AI requires an unusually high degree of coordination amongst tightly complementary pieces: compute, chips, data access, frontier research talent, model evaluation, cloud infrastructure, procurement, regulatory approval, defense deployment.

These pieces don't just benefit from being coordinated — they are useless without each other. A compute cluster without models is an expensive heater. A model without compute is a research paper. A research paper without deployment infrastructure is a citation. None of these things, alone, is sovereignty.

The US solves this with gigantic capital pools and huge firms running at massive losses. OpenAI burns billions per year. Google's AI division has a functionally unlimited budget drawn from search advertising. Meta builds AI infrastructure subsidized by social media profits. The cost of coordination inside these firms is close to zero because there is one decision-maker.

China solves it differently: giant firms with coordinated state control, massive technical talent pools educated in the same system, and a government that treats AI as industrial policy rather than market outcome.

Europe has neither. The EU has 27 member states, each with its own industrial policy, its own procurement rules, its own political incentives. It has lots of regulators, lots of diluted capital, lots of languages, lots of industrial players but no true giant, and zero sense of centralized execution.

This is not a technology problem. It is a Coase problem. The transaction costs of coordinating AI development across 27 sovereign nations, each with veto power over procurement, each with domestic champions to protect, each with elections to win — those transaction costs are so high that the rational outcome is to do nothing. And doing nothing means dependency.

"We are graduating all the engineers. We are starting all the companies, but then we're exporting talent to the US. We're exporting companies very cheaply." — Victor Englesson, EQT

The EU may be the place to start a company, but it has none of the resources to scale it. And in AI, if you can't scale, you don't exist.

Stockholm — The Nordic Ecosystem

Chapter III: The Swedish Question

To understand what Europe is losing, look at Stockholm. Not Berlin, not Paris — Stockholm. Per capita, it produces more billion-dollar tech companies than anywhere outside Silicon Valley. Spotify. Klarna. King. Mojang. iZettle. And now, a generation of AI companies that are doing something remarkable: they're building world-class products and then leaving.

Take 11x. Founded in London by Hasan Sukkar, an AI company building autonomous digital workers. By early 2025, 11x had raised over $50 million, was growing at triple-digit rates, and had built a product that Fortune 500 companies were adopting. It was, by every measure, exactly the kind of company Europe says it wants.

Then it moved its headquarters to San Francisco.

The reasons were not mysterious. Keith Fearon, 11x's VP of Growth, was direct about it: "The talent density in SF for AI is unmatched. Our customers are there. Our investors want us there. The regulatory clarity is better. At some point, you have to ask: what is Europe offering that's worth staying for?"

The answer, increasingly, is nothing. Not nothing in absolute terms — Europe offers quality of life, good healthcare, excellent education, reasonable cost of living. But for a company that needs to raise $200 million, recruit from the global top 0.1% of ML talent, and sell to enterprise customers who expect a US headquarters, those lifestyle advantages don't clear the bar.

Victor Englesson at EQT, one of Europe's largest tech investors, has watched this pattern repeat dozens of times. "The pipeline is extraordinary," he says. "We have the engineers, the researchers, the founders. What we don't have is the infrastructure to keep them. No sovereign compute at scale. No anchor customers who will bet on a European provider. No capital markets that value staying. So they leave."

The Stockholm ecosystem — the one that produced Spotify and Skype and MySQL — is now an export machine. It creates value and ships it to California. The founders get rich. The investors get returns. Europe gets a press release and a shrinking tax base.

This is not brain drain in the traditional sense. The engineers often stay in Europe physically. The corporate headquarters moves. The IP moves. The equity value moves. The capability stays, but the control leaves.

Each of these departures is individually rational. Collectively, they represent the slow dismantling of European AI capacity. Not through malice. Through gravity.

6.03.2026 — Brussels, Belgium — European Commission

Chapter IV: Brussels

Henna Virkkunen stepped to the podium at 10:15 AM. The Berlaymont auditorium was perhaps two-thirds full — respectable for a Tuesday policy address, though the empty seats in the back rows told their own story. Three camera crews. A handful of print journalists. The usual Brussels crowd: policy advisors, lobbyists, a few MEPs who had wandered over from committee meetings.

Virkkunen was the European Commission's Executive Vice-President for Tech Sovereignty, Security and Democracy. She had held the role since the previous autumn and had spent most of that time doing what Brussels does best: convening working groups, commissioning studies, drafting frameworks. Today was supposed to be different. Today, she had been told, was the speech that would "reset the narrative."

"Distinguished colleagues," she began. "The numbers are not encouraging. European share of global AI compute stands at approximately two percent. Our largest AI company, Mistral, holds approximately two percent of the global large language model market. Seventy percent of our cloud infrastructure is operated by three American companies. These are not projections. These are facts."

She paused. The room was attentive but not urgent. These numbers had been cited before, in various configurations, by various commissioners. The word sovereignty had appeared in over 340 official EU documents in the past three years. The word had done no work.

"I am announcing today the European AI Infrastructure Initiative," Virkkunen continued. "A coordinated investment framework to develop sovereign compute capacity, support European foundation model development, and create procurement pathways for European AI solutions."

She outlined the pillars. Five billion euros over five years. A network of "AI Excellence Centers" in twelve member states. Procurement preference guidelines for European providers. A talent retention fund. A compute sovereignty roadmap.

The applause was polite. A few nodded. The journalists typed. The MEPs checked their phones.

After the speech, in the corridor outside, Virkkunen walked with her chief of staff, Marek. They had thirty minutes before the next meeting.

"How was it?" she asked.

Marek was honest. "The speech was good. The problem is the math."

"What do you mean?"

"Five billion over five years is one billion per year. OpenAI alone will spend twelve billion this year on compute. Google will spend more than that. We are announcing, with great ceremony, an investment that is roughly eight percent of what a single American company spends annually."

Virkkunen stopped walking. "I know the math, Marek."

"I know you know. But does the room know? Did anyone in there react to the number like it was a problem? They applauded. They think five billion is a lot of money."

"It is a lot of money."

"It is a lot of money for a highway. It is not a lot of money for an AI sovereignty strategy. And calling it 'sovereign' doesn't make it sovereign. Where does the compute run? On what chips? Designed by whom? Manufactured where? We're going to build 'sovereign' AI infrastructure on NVIDIA GPUs, running on equipment from ASML that we can't even export freely, hosted in data centers operated by American cloud providers who will offer to 'partner' on the excellence centers because it's cheaper than building our own."

They walked in silence for a moment.

"So what do we do?" Virkkunen asked. Not rhetorically. She was asking.

Marek looked at her. "Something that can't be divided by twenty-seven."

    4.16.2027 — Berlin, Germany — Federal Chancellery

    Chapter V: Berlin

    Friedrich Merz was reading at 5 AM. This was not unusual — he had been a morning reader his entire career, a habit from his days as a corporate lawyer when the only quiet hours were the ones before the office woke up. What was unusual was what he was reading.

    The document was titled "Digital Liberty Act: Framework for Trusted AI Access Partnerships." It had arrived through diplomatic channels the previous evening, flagged by the BND with a single-line assessment: "This changes everything."

    Merz read it twice. The first time quickly, scanning for structure. The second time slowly, with a pencil, marking passages. The document was written in the particular style of American legislative drafting — precise in its vagueness, creating categories that appeared neutral but were designed to sort the world into those who comply and those who don't.

    The core mechanism was simple: access to American frontier AI systems — the models, the APIs, the cloud infrastructure, the enterprise tools built on top of them — would now be conditional. Not on payment. On alignment. Countries would be sorted into three tiers: Full Access, Managed Access, and Restricted. The criteria for placement included trade relationships, data governance alignment, technology transfer policies, and — the part Merz underlined three times — "demonstrated commitment to shared values in AI governance."

    Germany, with its deep trade relationships with China, its complicated position on data sovereignty, and its recent pushback on US cloud procurement, was projected to fall into Managed Access. This was not a ban. It was something worse: a leash. Managed Access meant delayed model releases, restricted API capabilities, mandatory data sharing agreements, and procurement conditions that would make German companies second-class customers of the AI infrastructure they depended on.

    At 6:30 AM, Pieter arrived. Pieter was Merz's foreign policy advisor, a quiet Dutchman who had spent fifteen years at the European Council before joining the Chancellery. He found Merz at his desk with the document spread across the table, annotated in red.

    "You've read it," Pieter said. Not a question.

    "Twice."

    "And?"

    Merz leaned back. "It's elegant. I'll give them that. They've found a way to weaponize dependency without firing a shot. Every German hospital running American diagnostic AI, every factory using American predictive maintenance, every bank using American fraud detection — all of it now has a political price attached."

    "We could comply," Pieter said carefully. "Restructure the China trade relationships. Signal alignment. Get back to Full Access."

    "At what cost? Our China trade is worth 250 billion euros annually. We're going to restructure that because an American technology company wants us to?"

    "It's not the company. It's the government."

    "It's both. That's the point. The government writes the tiers. The companies enforce them. And if we comply, what happens next time? They change the criteria. They always change the criteria."

    Pieter sat down. "So what's the alternative?"

    Merz was quiet for a long moment. Then: "Call Macron."

    "It's 6:45 in the morning."

    "He'll be awake. He reads the intelligence briefs at six. He'll have seen this."

    Pieter made the call. Macron had indeed seen it. The conversation lasted forty minutes. It was, by all later accounts, the first time the two leaders had spoken about AI as something other than an industrial policy footnote. They disagreed on almost everything — who should lead, how to structure it, what to build first. But they agreed on one thing: the era of assuming American AI infrastructure would remain apolitical was over.

    When Pieter hung up, Merz asked: "What did he say about the Nordics?"

    "He wants to call Ek."

    "Daniel Ek? The Spotify man?"

    "He's become the de facto voice of Nordic tech sovereignty. He has the capital, the credibility, and apparently he's been privately funding a European compute initiative for the past year."

    Merz looked at the document on his desk. "I've spent my entire career believing the transatlantic relationship was the foundation of European security. And it is. But this—" he tapped the Digital Liberty Act, "—this is not a partnership. This is a terms-of-service agreement. And we didn't negotiate the terms."

    Digital Liberty Act

    Framework for Trusted AI Access Partnerships

    Section 1. Purpose

    To establish a tiered framework for international access to United States artificial intelligence systems, infrastructure, and services, ensuring that such access aligns with the national security interests and values of the United States.

    Section 2. Access Tiers

    (a) Full Access. Nations demonstrating comprehensive alignment with US AI governance principles, including data sharing agreements, technology transfer transparency, and trade relationship compatibility, shall receive unrestricted access to frontier AI systems.

    (b) Managed Access. Nations maintaining significant trade or technology relationships with designated strategic competitors shall receive access subject to delayed model releases (90-180 days), restricted API capabilities, mandatory telemetry sharing, and enhanced procurement review.

    (c) Restricted Access. Nations failing to meet minimum alignment criteria shall be subject to comprehensive access limitations as determined by the Secretary of Commerce in consultation with the National Security Advisor.

    Section 3. Compliance Assessment Criteria

    The Secretary of Commerce shall evaluate partner nations annually based on: (i) bilateral trade composition with strategic competitors; (ii) data governance framework compatibility; (iii) technology transfer and export control alignment; (iv) demonstrated commitment to shared values in AI governance; (v) defense and intelligence cooperation metrics.

    Section 4. Enforcement

    United States AI service providers operating internationally shall implement access tier restrictions within 180 days of tier designation. Failure to comply shall result in penalties as prescribed under existing technology export frameworks.

    What Gets Built

    Chapter VI: The Coalition

    There is a version of this story where nothing happens. Where the Digital Liberty Act is absorbed, like every other external shock, into the vast sponge of European process. Where twenty-seven countries each write their own response. Where the coordination costs, as always, exceed the cost of inaction. Where Europe remains a consumer of AI rather than a producer of it.

    That version is the most likely one. It is also the most dangerous.

    The alternative requires something Europe has done exactly once before in technology: build an Airbus.

    The Airbus parallel is overused but under-examined. People cite it as proof that European industrial collaboration works. They forget how ugly it was. The first decade was a disaster of competing national interests, workshare politics, duplicated engineering, and political interference in technical decisions. France wanted final assembly in Toulouse. Germany wanted the fuselages built in Hamburg. The UK wanted the wings. Spain wanted the tail. Every country wanted the jobs. Nobody wanted to concede control.

    It worked anyway. Not because the collaboration was smooth, but because the alternative — letting Boeing own 100% of commercial aviation — was worse than the friction. The coalition was born from synchronized desperation, not synchronized ambition.

    The AI Version

    An AI coalition would not look exactly like Airbus. The technology is different. The timescales are shorter. The capital requirements are higher relative to revenue. But the structural logic is identical: merge the pieces that are too expensive to duplicate, keep the pieces that benefit from competition.

    The merged core: compute infrastructure, frontier model training, chip procurement, security certification. These are the capital-intensive, scale-dependent, winner-take-all layers. No individual European country can afford them alone. Together, they might.

    The competitive layer: enterprise applications, vertical solutions, research programs, talent development. These benefit from diversity, from multiple approaches, from the kind of messy experimentation that markets do better than committees.

    Think of it like the Wi-Fi Alliance. Nobody "owns" Wi-Fi. But the standard exists because companies agreed to share the infrastructure layer so they could compete on the application layer. The router is shared. The apps are competitive.

    Forks in the Road

    This coalition will face the same governance fights that Airbus faced. Who leads? Where is the headquarters? Whose procurement rules apply? How are costs shared? How is IP owned?

    These questions do not have clean answers. They have negotiated answers. And negotiated answers are slow, ugly, and suboptimal. But a suboptimal coalition that exists beats an optimal strategy that doesn't.

    The question is not whether Europe can build this. It has the talent, the capital (if aggregated), and the market size. The question is whether it will — whether the political cost of coordination is lower than the strategic cost of dependency. Whether, when the next crisis comes — and it will — there is a structure to respond within, or just twenty-seven countries each writing their own communique.

    The Argument

    Europe does not have a technology problem. It has a coordination problem. The talent exists. The capital exists, if aggregated. The market exists. What does not exist is a structure that can act at the speed and scale that AI requires.

    The Airbus precedent is not a guarantee. It is proof of concept. It shows that European countries can, under sufficient pressure, build industrial champions that compete with American incumbents. It also shows that it takes a decade of ugly compromises to get there.

    The question is whether Europe starts now — imperfectly, messily, with all the political friction that entails — or waits until the dependency is so deep that the cost of building an alternative exceeds the cost of accepting the terms.

    The slow boil is already underway. The only question is whether anyone turns off the heat.
