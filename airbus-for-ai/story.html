Prologue — Economics 101

Why Do Firms Exist?

Go back to the source. Ronald Coase: firms exist when it is cheaper to organize activity inside a hierarchy than through constant market bargaining.

This is the central sin of the middle powers. AI requires an unusually high degree of coordination amongst tightly complementary pieces: compute, chips, data access, frontier research talent, model evaluation, cloud infrastructure, procurement, regulatory approval, defense deployment.

The US solves this with gigantic capital pools and huge firms running at massive losses. China has giant firms with coordinated state control and a truly huge font of technical talent.

✈ THE AIRBUS PARALLEL
In the late 1960s, Boeing owned commercial aviation. Individual European manufacturers were all too small to compete alone. Each had capability, but none had the full stack.

The economics of wide-body aircraft made it impossible to compete at national scale. France, Germany, the UK, and Spain didn't want to merge their aerospace industries. They did it because the alternative was worse. The first decade was full of fights over workshare, headquarters, whose engine gets used. It worked despite the friction.

"We are graduating all the engineers. We are starting all the companies, but then we're exporting talent to the US. We're exporting companies very cheaply." — Victor Englesson, EQT

The EU may be the place to start a company, but it has none of the resources to scale it.

✈ THE AIRBUS PARALLEL
By 1975 Airbus had sold 38 planes. Boeing sold over 1,500 that decade. The early years were humiliating. But the structure held.

The A300 was a commercial disappointment for its first five years. Airlines wouldn't buy European. But the consortium kept iterating, and by the 1980s had real customers. Today Airbus holds ~50% of the global commercial aviation market. The question was never "is it as good on day one?" It was "does the structure survive long enough to get good?"
